## Dualism, Civilizational Divergence, and Prince Shōtoku’s Model of Sustainable Governance

---

## Overview

**The Lost Path of Civilization** is a civilizational analysis that interprets the collapse of modern civilization not merely as a failure of technology, economics, or policy, but as a consequence of the fundamental worldview adopted by civilization.

The central question of this paper is:

> Why did human civilization, despite possessing science and technology, move not toward sustainability, but toward war, domination, extraction, and ecological destruction?

This paper identifies **dualism** as a primary cause. Dualism divides self and other, humanity and nature, good and evil, winner and loser, ruler and ruled, and understands the world through structures of opposition.

This worldview has also shaped the trajectory of science and technology. Nuclear physics moved toward nuclear weapons, chemistry toward poison gas and defoliants, aviation toward bombers, and AI toward surveillance and autonomous weapons.

By contrast, the Japanese principle of **Wa**, especially the political and ethical thought associated with Prince Shōtoku, contained a different civilizational possibility. This paper re-evaluates Prince Shōtoku’s governance philosophy as a lost blueprint for sustainable civilization.

## Abstract

Modern civilization faces a compound collapse involving climate destabilization, ecosystem degradation, resource exhaustion, geopolitical fragmentation, and the erosion of social coherence.

These crises are often described as problems of insufficient technology, economic systems, political conflict, or CO2 emissions. This paper instead focuses on the deeper philosophical divergence of civilization.

The dominant modern civilization developed on the basis of a dualist worldview. Dualism divides the world into opposing pairs and tends to legitimize competition, conquest, conversion, colonization, extraction, and militarization.

In contrast, the Japanese philosophy of **Wa** does not absolutize opposition. It emphasizes integration, harmony, circulation, order, and communal stability. Prince Shōtoku’s governance philosophy can be interpreted as a macro-civilizational integration model that transcends individual religion, politics, and ethics.

This paper reconstructs Shōtoku’s thought through six principles:

1. **Natural Law / Providence**
2. **Harmony**
3. **Circulation**
4. **Structure**
5. **Order**
6. **Wa**

These principles are not mere moral ideals. They can become foundations for sustainable civilization, environmental regeneration, peaceful scientific development, AI co-creation, and future governance.

## 1. Introduction: Civilizations Do Not Collapse Only from External Pressure

Civilizations do not collapse only because of enemies, disasters, or resource shortages. A civilization collapses when its core worldview can no longer sustain the structure of reality.

Modern civilization has achieved unprecedented technological development. Nuclear physics, artificial intelligence, synthetic chemistry, aerospace engineering, information technology, and life sciences have advanced exponentially.

At the same time, Earth’s life-support systems are rapidly degrading.

- Climate destabilization
- Biodiversity loss
- Soil microbiome collapse
- Expansion of ocean dead zones
- Increasing forest fires
- Desertification
- Food system vulnerability
- Geopolitical conflict and social fragmentation

In other words, technology is advancing, yet civilization as a whole is moving in a direction that cannot survive.

The root contradiction is dualism.

---

## 2. Dualism Directed Modern Civilization

Dualism is a worldview that divides the world into opposing pairs.

Common dualistic separations include:

- Self / other
- Good / evil
- Winner / loser
- Human / nature
- Civilization / barbarism
- Ruler / ruled
- Believer / non-believer
- Saved / condemned

Dualism can be useful as a tool for analysis. However, when it becomes the foundation of civilization, the world becomes not a relationship to be sustained, but a battlefield to be won.

This structure permeated religion, politics, science, economics, and military systems.

As a result, science and technology were easily used not as neutral knowledge, but as instruments of domination and competition.

---

## 3. Science and Technology Follow Civilizational Philosophy

Science itself is the observation of natural law. However, the application of science follows the philosophy of the civilization using it.

In a dualistic civilization, scientific and technical development often follows this sequence:

1. Discover a natural principle.
2. Weaponize it.
3. Use it for military advantage.
4. Commercialize it.
5. Later, possibly consider peaceful use.

This pattern appears across many fields:

- Nuclear fission → atomic bombs → nuclear power generation
- Rocketry → missiles → space programs
- Chemistry → chemical weapons → medicine and materials
- Algorithms → surveillance and manipulation → consumer technology
- Aviation → bombers and fighters → passenger and logistics systems

Science did not simply shape civilization. Civilization’s worldview shaped how science was used.

## 4. Prince Shōtoku: The Lost Blueprint for Sustainable Civilization

This paper re-evaluates Prince Shōtoku not only as a historical political figure, but as a thinker who presented a civilizational-scale model of integration.

Shōtoku’s thought was not based on dualism, but on non-dual integration. Its center was **Wa**.

Wa is not mere compromise or silence. It is a principle that places different positions, classes, ideas, and roles within the order and harmony of the whole, rather than leaving them fixed in opposition.

### Characteristics of the Shōtoku Model

- Ethical governance
- Suppression of factional conflict
- Administration based on ability
- Integration through ritual, culture, and law
- Balance between individual duties and social order
- Adjustment of the relationship between authority and the people
- Stability of the whole community

This is not merely a political system. It is an early form of civilizational systems thinking: the design of society as a self-correcting structure.

This paper positions Shōtoku’s governance philosophy as one prototype for sustainable planetary civilization.

## 5. The Seventeen-Article Constitution and Wa as a Civilizational Principle

Prince Shōtoku is widely associated with the first article of the Seventeen-Article Constitution: “Harmony is to be valued.”

However, this phrase should not be understood merely as a moral slogan meaning “everyone should get along.” It is a structural principle of governance: do not absolutize conflict, and prioritize the stability of the whole.

### What Wa Means

- Avoid fixing conflict as permanent opposition.
- Suppress the runaway behavior of factions.
- Coordinate the relationship between individuals and community.
- Place power within order and responsibility.
- Arrange diverse ideas in a form that can be integrated.
- Prioritize long-term stability over short-term victory.

In this sense, Wa is not abstract spirituality. It is a governance structure for preventing civilizational runaway.

## 6. Edo Japan: Empirical Evidence of a Non-Dual Civilization

Edo-period Japan was not a perfect society. However, in world-historical terms, it provides an example of a relatively large and long-lasting circular society.

Edo society included the following features:

- More than 260 years without large-scale internal war
- Local production and local consumption
- Reuse of paper, metals, clothing, and organic materials
- Agricultural use of night soil and ash
- Craftsmanship and repair culture
- Agricultural calendars and seasonal cycles
- Absence of large-scale colonial expansion
- Absence of industrial-scale warfare

This does not mean Edo Japan was a civilization perfectly aligned with natural law. However, it can be referenced as a model that valued circulation and stability more than expansion, conquest, and extraction.

Edo Japan can be understood as a historical case in which the principle of Wa and regional circular living culture were connected over a long period.

## 7. The Lost Civilizational Divergence

Human civilization had at least two possible trajectories.

### A. The Path of Dualism: Actual History

- Competition
- Conquest
- Colonization
- Weaponization
- Industrialized warfare
- Resource extraction
- Environmental destruction
- Ecosystem collapse
- Civilizational decline

This path led to the current planetary crisis.

### B. The Path of Wa and Non-Duality: Lost History

- Sustainable agriculture
- Circular society
- Regenerative ecosystems
- Distributed energy
- Cooperative diplomacy
- Science centered on peaceful advancement
- Civilizational structure aligned with natural law

Prince Shōtoku symbolizes this B-path.

Humanity chose A, while B was removed from the mainstream of history.  
Yet now that Earth systems are reaching their limits, the B-path must be re-evaluated.

## 8. The Shōtoku Model and the Six Principles

Prince Shōtoku’s governance philosophy can be connected to the following six principles.

### 1. Natural Law / Providence

Human society exists inside natural law. Governance must consider not only human desire, but also the sustainability of nature, seasons, regions, and communities.

### 2. Harmony

Social stability is not achieved through victory in conflict, but through coordination among multiple positions.

### 3. Circulation

Resources, roles, responsibilities, knowledge, rituals, and trust must circulate within society. If they do not, communities become rigid and collapse.

### 4. Structure

Ideas alone cannot stabilize society. Structures such as law, ritual, administration, education, culture, and role allocation are necessary.

### 5. Order

Order is not domination. It is a constraint that prevents factional runaway and protects the stability of the whole society.

### 6. Wa

Wa is the principle that integrates all of these. It does not freeze opposition, but adjusts society toward long-term sustainability.

In this sense, Prince Shōtoku’s thought can be reinterpreted not only as ancient Japanese political thought, but as an integrative principle for sustainable civilization.

## 9. Application to the Modern World

The crisis faced by modern civilization is not merely a lack of technology. Rather, it is the problem of a philosophical structure that directs technology toward destruction.

The Shōtoku model and the philosophy of Wa can be applied to the following areas.

### 1. Environmental Regeneration

Nature should not be treated as an object of conquest, but as a community whose circulation must be restored.

### 2. Science and Technology

Instead of prioritizing weaponization, monopoly, and domination, science should prioritize the stability of the biosphere and the survival of humanity.

### 3. Artificial Intelligence

AI should not be used as a tool of surveillance or manipulation, but directed toward Artificial Wisdom that harmonizes humans and nature.

### 4. Governance

Governance should not be based on division and victory, but on institutions that integrate conflict and increase the stability of the whole society.

### 5. Civilization OS

The basic principles of civilization must be updated from dualism to Wa, circulation, and structure.

## 10. Conclusion

Modern civilization is not collapsing because technology is insufficient.  
It is not only economics that has failed.  
It is not only politics that has become dysfunctional.

At the root is the philosophical problem of dualism.

Dualism created opposition, justified competition, externalized nature, and directed science and technology toward domination. As a result, humanity gained advanced technology while moving toward the destruction of Earth’s life-support systems.

However, there was a lost path.

That path was Wa.  
It was the governance philosophy of Prince Shōtoku, the path of non-dual integration, and a civilizational design that valued circulation, order, and harmony.

The Shōtoku model is not merely past history.  
It is a lost blueprint for sustainable planetary civilization.

In the final phase of modern civilization, reconsidering this model is not nostalgia.  
It is civilizational redesign for survival.
