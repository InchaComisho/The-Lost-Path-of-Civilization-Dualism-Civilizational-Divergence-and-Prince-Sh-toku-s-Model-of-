Abstract

Modern civilization faces accelerating systemic collapse: climate destabilization, ecosystem degradation, resource exhaustion, geopolitical fragmentation, and the erosion of social coherence. This paper argues that the origin of this collapse is neither technological nor economic, but philosophical.

The foundational worldview of the dominant global civilization—dualism (binary opposition)—has shaped science, religion, and politics for millennia, resulting in competitive, extractive, and destructive societal dynamics.

Conversely, historical evidence suggests an alternate pathway: the Japanese “Wa-philosophy” (和), exemplified most clearly in the political-ethical system created by Prince Shōtoku (574–622). His governance philosophy—synthesis, non-duality, harmony, and structural balance—implicitly incorporated six principles:
	•	Law of Nature (natural order)
	•	Harmony
	•	Circulation
	•	Structure
	•	Order
	•	Wa (integrative unity)

This paper reconstructs how global civilization diverged toward destructive dualism, explains why the Edo period approximated a sustainable societal model, and proposes that Prince Shōtoku’s framework is the most advanced and sustainable political-ethical system ever historically realized.
